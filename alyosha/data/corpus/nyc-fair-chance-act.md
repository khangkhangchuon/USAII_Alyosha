# NYC Fair Chance Act — your rights when applying for a job

The NYC Fair Chance Act protects job applicants in New York City from being
asked about their criminal history before getting a job offer.

## Key rule: criminal history can't be part of the hiring process until *after*
## a conditional job offer

Employers cannot ask about your criminal history — or run a background check —
**until after they have made you a conditional offer**.

## Employer obligations (4+ employees)

Unless an exception applies, an employer with four or more employees must:

1. **Wait** until after a conditional offer before requesting your criminal
   records.
2. **Conduct an individualized assessment** weighing the "fair chance factors"
   (such as the nature of the offense, time since the offense, age at the
   time, and the job in question).
3. **Give you a copy** of that written assessment and a copy of the background
   check before taking any adverse action (such as withdrawing the offer).
4. **Hold the position open** for at least **5 business days** so you can
   respond and address any concerns.

## What you can do if an offer is withdrawn

If the employer decides to withdraw your offer because of your record, they
must put their reasoning in writing and give you a copy of the background
check. You have time (generally 5 business days) to respond — for example,
to correct errors in the report or explain context the employer should
consider.

## How to file a complaint

If you believe an employer violated these rules, you can:

- **Call 311** and ask for the Commission on Human Rights.
- **Leave an anonymous tip** with the Commission on Human Rights.
- **File a complaint** about what happened.

The NYC Commission on Human Rights (CCHR) enforces the Fair Chance Act.

## Important limit

The Fair Chance Act protects you in **employment**. It does not provide legal
advice for your specific situation, and it doesn't cover every workplace
(some employers and roles are exempt). If you think your rights were violated
in a specific job offer or a background check, contact the Legal Aid Society
Reentry Unit or the Commission on Human Rights for help with your case.
